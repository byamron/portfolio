# Screenless TV: Designing for Shared Reality

## The quick version

Vision concept for Sony's TV division. My team explored how immersive technology could shape home entertainment — and found that the real design challenge wasn't personalization, it was preserving the shared experience that makes watching together meaningful.

## Timeline

Q1 – Q3 2024

## People don't watch together for the content — they watch together for the connection

Master's capstone, 2024. Sony's TV division asked us to explore how AR/MR might shape home entertainment. We interviewed young adults expecting to hear about picture quality. Instead we heard about compromise: subtitles or not, volume levels, lighting preferences. Someone always loses.

Despite the compromises, people kept watching together. The value isn't the content — it's laughing at the same moment, discussing afterward, being present together. Current technology makes that connection harder, not easier. The design challenge was clear: give each person what they need without forcing everyone to settle on the same thing.

## Wearables solve personalization by killing the thing that matters

Apple Vision Pro had just launched. Wearables were the obvious path — personalized visuals and audio for each viewer. Technically feasible, proven model, industry momentum. We debated it seriously.

But our research kept pulling us back: the reason people watch together is presence. You can't catch someone's eye during a tense scene through a headset. Even at the smallest form factor, there's something between you and your people. We weren't designing separate realities optimized for each person. We were preserving a shared reality that works for everyone. This is a values decision, not a technical one. That's the point.

## Screenless TV: shared space, personalized experience

A volumetric display projected from a flat device — no screen on the wall, no headset. The display appears when you want it and disappears when you don't. Angle-specific imagery means you see subtitles in your language while your partner sees theirs. Directional audio delivers different volumes to different positions. Personalization without isolation.

## Users didn't care how it worked — they cared that it kept them together

We tested through video prototypes and AR simulations. Participants connected with the disappearing moment and with familiar touchpoints like a remote control that grounded novel technology in something they already understood. But the strongest response wasn't to any specific feature — it was to the idea that technology could facilitate togetherness instead of fragmenting it.
