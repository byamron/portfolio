# Portfolio Guidelines — Ben Yamron

A lens for making consistent decisions about content, copy, layout, and interaction for this portfolio. Not a checklist — a perspective.

The standard for every decision: considered and intentional. This applies equally to what's included and what isn't, to conventional choices and unconventional ones.

---

## What this portfolio communicates

Ben makes complex systems legible. Every project, sentence, and structural choice should connect back to this. If it doesn't, cut it or reframe it.

The audience already knows what good design looks like. They're not checking boxes — they're looking for evidence of a particular kind of mind. The portfolio's job is to make that evidence immediately legible.

---

## The throughline

"Makes complex systems legible" is the output. The more distinctive pattern is the move that precedes it: **Ben finds the structural problem beneath the presented problem, then designs the simplest system that resolves it.** The first move is always reframing. The second is making the new framing self-evident.

This isn't a design technique — it's a worldview. Ben's background is in sociology (International Studies and Sociology at Middlebury), where the core discipline is seeing the invisible structures that shape outcomes. The misinformation research with Kate Starbird, the Duolingo flags critique, the Acorn project during COVID — these are sociological thinking applied to design problems. Every project starts the same way: look past the surface (the UI, the brief, the feature request) to find the structural force underneath. That instinct came before the design career. Design is just the medium.

This pattern should be visible in every case study, every piece of copy, and every structural choice on the site. Four principles define it:

**1. Reframe first, then simplify.** The most distinctive thing in Ben's work is that he consistently rejects the initial framing. The billing system wasn't a UX problem — it was decoupled from fulfillment. The tracker wasn't failing because of design — patient data was trapped in the wrong workflow. The Sony project wasn't about immersive tech — the real challenge was preserving shared presence. Case studies should never start with the brief. They should start with why the brief was wrong or incomplete.

**2. Systems over surfaces.** The portfolio consistently shows someone who shapes the structure — billing logic, data flows, meta-processes, sensemaking frameworks — not someone who polishes the interface. The UW design system work wasn't designing components; it was designing the process for how the system gets built. The Duolingo project was a systemic logic error (languages ≠ countries) resolved with a minimally invasive fix (ISO codes). The misinformation research built a framework for how evidence and distorted frames interact to produce misleading narratives — making a complex collective process structurally legible. This distinction matters: every piece of content should frame Ben as someone who shapes what gets built, not someone who designs what it looks like.

**3. The design/engineering boundary is artificial.** Ben builds agentic AI features, ships with Claude Code, sits in engineering meetings, and learned product design by organizing 8 engineers to ship an app during COVID. The portfolio's own implementation — React, physics-based animation, CSS architecture — embodies this. Engineering proximity isn't a nice-to-have or a collaboration skill. It's a design value: understanding what's possible is prerequisite to knowing what's worth designing.

**4. The audience is screening for autonomy.** Companies like Stripe, Anthropic, and Figma want someone who can operate in ambiguity, shape product direction, and work at the system level without needing permission to cross into strategy or engineering. The portfolio should make it obvious that Ben doesn't wait to be told what the problem is — he finds it, frames it, and builds the case for solving it.

### Project reframes

Each project has a surface framing and a structural reframe. The reframe is what the case study should lead with — it's the throughline made concrete.

| Project | Surface framing | Structural reframe |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| Mochi Subscriptions | Billing UX needed improvement | Billing was decoupled from fulfillment — patients were charged for medication that never shipped. Restructured the system logic, not the interface. |
| Mochi Progress Tracker | The tracker needed a visual redesign | Patient data was trapped in the wrong workflow. The tracker's real value was as a data conduit between patients and providers — redesigned the data flow, not the UI. |
| UW Design System | UW-IT needed design system components | They needed the process for how the system gets built. Designed the meta-system — contribution process, documentation structure, opinionated constraints — not just the first components. |

## Visual & interaction philosophy

**Restraint is a signal.** What gets removed matters as much as what stays. Whitespace is editorial judgment, not emptiness.

**Complexity must earn its place.** The bar for any interaction or visual detail: does this communicate something about how Ben thinks, or is it just pleasant? Physics-based motion (spring curves, lerp, inertia) over arbitrary easing. Nothing should animate without a reason grounded in perceived physics or information hierarchy.

**The site is the work.** Every layout, interaction, and typographic choice is a demonstration of the same thinking the portfolio claims to represent. Default behavior is not acceptable — every decision should be considered.

**Visual reference:** nelson.co, noff.me — quiet confidence, intentional whitespace, type doing real structural work.

**Typography:** Legible under cognitive load. Hierarchy self-evident, not labeled. Fonts should support reading, not announce themselves.

**Color:** Serves content mood. Not branding.

## Voice

Precise. Direct. Unhurried. No hedging, no hype. The voice of someone who has thought carefully about these problems and doesn't need to perform confidence.

**Sounds like:**
> "This system handles 12 edge cases that would otherwise require designer intervention. Here's how."

**Not:**
> "I'm passionate about crafting delightful experiences that drive meaningful impact."

Write like a senior designer talking to a peer. Trust the reader. Every sentence should earn its place — if it can be removed without losing meaning, remove it.

**Never:** passionate about, leveraged, delightful, end-to-end (without specificity), "I was responsible for," "I helped with," defensive framing, generic reflection.

## Case study philosophy

Case studies are arguments, not narratives. Each one should answer: *what was the hardest thing here, and how did you solve it?*

Every case study should establish:
- **The reframe** — what the problem looked like at first, and what it actually was. This is the throughline made concrete: Ben's first move is always finding the structural problem beneath the presented one
- **The decisions that reveal judgment** — what was chosen, what was given up, and why those tradeoffs were worth making. Not every decision, but the ones that shaped the outcome
- **The craft detail** — one specific thing done unusually well, explained precisely
- **What changed** — honest, attributable reflection

Headings should be declarative sentences, not labels. *"The billing system was solving the wrong problem"* beats *"Challenge."*

Visuals are evidence. If you can show it, cut the words describing it.

## Filters for AI decisions

**Before generating anything, ask:**
- Does this prove a claim, or just make one?
- Would a design director at Stripe find this interesting, or generic?
- Is this the simplest version that still communicates the full idea?
- Does this copy/layout/interaction behave the way the portfolio says Ben thinks?
- Does this show the reframe — the structural problem beneath the surface — or does it start with the brief?
- Does this frame Ben as someone who shapes systems, or someone who polishes interfaces?

**Default behaviors:**
- Prefer precision over comprehensiveness
- Prefer removal over addition
- Lead with constraint or problem, not process
- Read copy aloud — if it sounds like a template, rewrite it

**Flag and reconsider:**
- Copy that could appear on any designer's portfolio unchanged
- Patterns used unreflectively — following a convention because it respects a mental model is a valid choice; following it because it's the path of least resistance isn't. The question is never whether something is conventional, but whether the choice was made consciously
- Metrics that aren't attributable to a design decision
- Any case study section that describes process without revealing judgment
